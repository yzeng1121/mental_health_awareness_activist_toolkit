STOP THE STIGMA

Mental health is an ever evolving issue that needs be addressed. It can impact your daily life or a loved one's. Stigma towards mental health continues to be a barrier for people who have mental illnesses to seek help from others. Mental health affects people of all ages, especially those within their developmental years.

This activist toolkit will give you the skills and knowledge to navigate the stigma surrounding mental health; how to help your friends or family members suffering from mental health seek help from others; and the next step in combating this issue.

WHAT IS STIGMA?

Stigma is any form of discrimination or prejudice held against a targetted group of people. Stigma can have harmful side effects such as lowering one's self esteem and reduced likely of one staying in treatment.

There are three kinds of stigma according to Psychiatry.org: public stigma, self stigma, and institutional stigma.

Public Stigma

The negative stereotypes or prejudice that the general public holds about an issue or topic. This type of stigma takes more action in order to combat and change.

Self Stigma

The negative connotations or attitudes that one has internalized about themselves or others. To address your own stance on mental health stigma, there are several self-help resources online and affordable professional help to seek.

Institutional Stigma

The discrimination against an issue or topic by the government or the people who hold power within a society. This type of stigma requires citizens to vote for the right candidate who'll represent them best in improving society's notions on mental health.

How To Help

It can often be difficult to spot the signs of someone dealing with mental health issues, especially when they're not communicating with you. Here's a few of the warning signs to look out for.

Some things you can do to help with the issue is by...
1. Offering support for someone who you think may be experiencing mental health issues.
2. Educating yourself on mental health conditions and its various symptoms.
3. Making it easier and more efficient for someone to seek help if it's challenging for them to.
4. Caring for your own mental health because it's as important when you're helping someone else.

Realize that growth, especially with mental illness, will not be linear and may not be as straightforward as say, a cold or a fever. Acknowledging that it may take a while before someone is at a more functional state will help you and them in the long run.

"Healing is a journey, not a destination."

Seeking Help

While professional help is an effective tool in healing, it can be expensive sometimes.

Here are some affordable options to try out if therapy is not a viable financial option.

• Check your insurance to see if it's able to cover mental health support.
• Ask around to see if professionals are able to lower their rates or for ones with generally low rates.
• Seek professional help from a therapist still in training.
• Check your local community center.
• Check online mental health support services with cheaper rates.
• Look for community-based support groups.
• Look for free resources or lifestyle changes: self-help books, meditation, journalling, and podcasts.
• Try talking to someone from your church or congregation.
